# Focused and Diffused Thinking

Focused and Diffused Thinking are two modes of Thinking that activate the prefrontal cortex, located at the front of the brain's frontal lobe. This part of the brain is responsible for cognitive control functions such as attention, decision-making, and reaction. [^1]

Focused Thinking engages the prefrontal cortex intensively, leading to intentional and focused thinking and learning. Just as the ball in a pinball machine follows a specific path through bumpers, your thoughts also follow a specific path in a logical and organized manner. [^1]

Diffused Thinking engages the prefrontal cortex more relaxedly, leading to spontaneous and creative thinking and problem-solving. It's like a lightbulb that spreads light in all directions, allowing you to consider information and ideas from various perspectives, often leading to innovation. [^1]

# Procrastination

Procrastination is a habit that can be difficult to break and shares similarities with addiction. It occurs when we are faced with tasks we do not like, which makes our brain associate the task with discomfort and subconciously looks for ways to avoid it to something we perceive more pleasant.  Procrastination can have negative long-term effects, such as making it more difficult to learn and retain information. To overcome procrastination, it is important to understand the routine habitual responses that lead to it and to develop strategies for dealing with it. Willpower should not be wasted on fending off procrastination unless absolutely necessary, and it is possible to trick habitual responses into helping fend off procrastination. 
1. Starting earlier and spacing out learning can lead to better outcomes than cramming.
2. Using the Pomodoro technique, which involves setting a timer and working intently and without distractions for 25 minutes and rewarding yourself after with simple things like "talking a walk for 5 minutes".
3. Change underlying beliefs about "habits"

# Human Memory Systems

Memory can be divided into two systems: working memory and long-term memory. Working memory is the immediate and conscious processing of information and has limited capacity, while long-term memory has vast storage capacity but requires repeated visits to effectively store information. Working memory is like a mental blackboard, holding only a few things in mind, while long-term memory is a storage warehouse for different kinds of memories. To transfer information from working memory to long-term memory, it takes time and practice. The technique of spaced repetition can help with this process. To form a good memory structure, the synaptic connections need time to form and strengthen.

On week 3, Barbara Oakley delved deeper into the topic of Memory. According to research, our brains have outstanding visual and spatial memory systems that can aid in forming long-term memories. The video suggests using memorable visual images to help remember concepts, as images connect directly to the right brain's visual spatial centers. Repetition is also important for moving information from working memory to long-term memory, and handwriting and saying information aloud can help enhance retention. Index cards can be a helpful tool for repetition and testing oneself on what has been learned. Gradually increasing spacing between repetitions can help lock material more firmly into long-term memory.

The key to memorization is to create meaningful groups that simplify the material, such as using mnemonics or memorable sentences. The memory palace technique is a powerful way to group unrelated items that you want to remember. Using memory tricks and innovative techniques helps to build neural hooks for creativity and expand working memory. These techniques have been shown to speed up the acquisition of knowledge and improve test performance. Memorizing key aspects of the material helps to understand it more deeply and apply it more proficiently in real-world situations.

# Importance of Sleep

Sleep is crucial for the brain to flush out toxins and consolidate memories, strengthening neural connections and promoting cognitive function, problem-solving, and understanding of complex ideas. A lack of sleep can lead to health problems, including headaches, depression, heart disease, and diabetes, and can even shorten lifespan. Dreaming about what you're studying can enhance understanding by consolidating memories. Getting enough sleep is essential for a healthy brain and overall well-being. Lack of sleep impairs performance and learning. Therefore, it is crucial to prioritize sleep and avoid taking tests with little sleep. Sleep is vital for learning and changing the brain's structure, and any degree of sleep deprivation will impair performance and learning.


# Chunking

Chunking is the mental leap that helps you unite bits of information together through meaning. This helps to create a neural network of neurons that fire together, allowing for smooth and effective thought or action. Repetition and structured practice help to create strong memory traces, leading to the creation of chunks and ultimately the path to expertise. Chunks make the brain run more efficiently, as they allow for the main idea to be remembered without having to recall all the underlying details.

When you’re first trying to understand how to work a problem, you have a heavy cognitive load. So, it helps to start out with a work-through example. It’s like first listening to a song before trying to play the song yourself. As you gain more experience in chunking in any particular subject, you’ll see that the chunks you’re able to create are bigger, in some sense that the ribbons are longer.

## A Library of Chunks

Keeping a library of chunked knowledge, or valuable bits of information that can be pieced together in new and creative ways, is important for developing creative flexibility in learning and problem-solving. The ability to combine chunks in new and original ways underlies a lot of historical innovation. As the number of chunks in your mind grows and becomes more well-practiced, the more easily you will be able to solve problems and figure out solutions. Chunks also help you understand new concepts and enable transfer of knowledge between different fields. A good library of chunks can help you connect two or more chunks together in new ways to solve novel problems. The focused mode involves sequential thinking where each small step leads deliberately towards a solution, while intuition involves a more holistic approach. The law of Serendipity states that just by focusing on whatever section you're studying, the first problem or concept will go in a little more easily, and the second and third even more easily.

## Illusions of Competence

Illusions of competence in learning refer to the false belief that one has understood and learned the material just because they have read it or glanced at it. Such illusions occur when students believe that they have learned the material just because they have the book or Google open in front of them, which can lead to ineffective studying. Jeffrey Karpicke's research has shown that recalling information is a more effective way of learning than simply rereading it.

## Overlearning, Choking, Einstellung, Chunking, and Interleaving
* Overlearning: Refers to continued practice or study of a skill or task even after mastery has been achieved, with the aim of enhancing retention and automaticity.
- Choking: Occurs when an individual's performance on a task is negatively impacted by pressure or anxiety, leading to a breakdown in their abilities despite having previously demonstrated competence.
- Einstellung: Refers to the phenomenon where a person's existing mental set or preconceived notions can hinder their ability to find more effective or creative solutions to a problem.
- Chunking: In the context of memory and learning, chunking involves breaking down information into smaller, more manageable units, which can improve memory recall and make it easier to process and retain information.
-  Interleaving: An instructional approach that involves alternating between different types of practice or tasks, rather than practicing one skill or task repeatedly in isolation. This method has been found to improve learning and retention by enhancing transfer of learning and promoting greater cognitive flexibility.
